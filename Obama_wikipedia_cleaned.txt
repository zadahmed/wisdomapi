%&|<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Barack Obama</h1>
<p><b>Barack Hussein Obama II</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px 'goose'">uː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span> <span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode and politician who served as the 44th <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> from 2009 to 2017. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>, he was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> President of the United States. He previously served as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._senator" title="U.S. senator">U.S. senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> from 2005 to 2008 and an <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Senate" title="Illinois Senate">Illinois state senator</a> from 1997 to 2004.
</p><p>Obama was born in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in 1983, he worked as a community organizer in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. In 1988, he enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a>, where he was the first black president of the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_Review" title="Harvard Law Review">Harvard Law Review</a></i>. After graduating, he became a civil rights attorney and an academic, teaching constitutional law at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_School" title="University of Chicago Law School">University of Chicago Law School</a> from 1992 to 2004. He <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Senate_career_of_Barack_Obama" title="Illinois Senate career of Barack Obama">represented the 13th district for three terms</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Senate" title="Illinois Senate">Illinois Senate</a> from 1997 until 2004, when he <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Illinois,_2004" title="United States Senate election in Illinois, 2004">ran for the U.S. Senate</a>. He received national attention in 2004 with his March primary win, his well-received July <a href="/wiki/2004_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2004 Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Convention</a> <a href="/wiki/2004_Democratic_National_Convention_keynote_address" title="2004 Democratic National Convention keynote address">keynote address</a>, and his landslide November election to the Senate. In 2008, he was nominated for president a year after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_presidential_campaign,_2008" title="Barack Obama presidential campaign, 2008">his campaign</a> began, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_presidential_primaries,_2008" title="Democratic Party presidential primaries, 2008">a close primary campaign</a> against <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a>. He was <a href="/wiki/2008_United_States_presidential_election" title="2008 United States presidential election">elected</a> over <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> and was <a href="/wiki/First_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="First inauguration of Barack Obama">inaugurated</a> on January 20, 2009. Nine months later, he was named the <a href="/wiki/2009_Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="2009 Nobel Peace Prize">2009 Nobel Peace Prize</a> laureate.
</p><p>Regarded as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrist" title="Centrist">centrist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Democrat" title="New Democrat">New Democrat</a>, Obama signed many landmark bills into law during his first two years in office. The main reforms that were passed include the <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act</a> (commonly referred to as the "Affordable Care Act" or "Obamacare"), the <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Wall_Street_Reform_and_Consumer_Protection_Act" title="Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act">Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Ask,_Don%27t_Tell_Repeal_Act_of_2010" title="Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010">Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010</a>. The <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_Relief,_Unemployment_Insurance_Reauthorization,_and_Job_Creation_Act_of_2010" title="Tax Relief, Unemployment Insurance Reauthorization, and Job Creation Act of 2010">Tax Relief, Unemployment Insurance Reauthorization, and Job Creation Act of 2010</a> served as economic stimulus amidst the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>. After a lengthy debate over the national debt limit, he signed the <a href="/wiki/Budget_Control_Act_of_2011" title="Budget Control Act of 2011">Budget Control</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Taxpayer_Relief_Act_of_2012" title="American Taxpayer Relief Act of 2012">American Taxpayer Relief Acts</a>. In foreign policy, he increased U.S. troop levels in <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Afghanistan</a>, reduced nuclear weapons with the United States–Russia <a href="/wiki/New_START" title="New START">New START</a> treaty, and <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_of_U.S._troops_from_Iraq" title="Withdrawal of U.S. troops from Iraq">ended military involvement</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>. He ordered <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">military involvement in Libya</a>, contributing to the overthrow of <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>. He also ordered the military operations that resulted in <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Osama_bin_Laden" title="Death of Osama bin Laden">the deaths of Osama bin Laden</a> and suspected Yemeni <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">Al-Qaeda</a> operative <a href="/wiki/Anwar_al-Awlaki" title="Anwar al-Awlaki">Anwar al-Awlaki</a>.
</p><p>After winning <a href="/wiki/2012_United_States_presidential_election" title="2012 United States presidential election">re-election</a> by defeating Republican opponent <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a>, Obama was <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="Second inauguration of Barack Obama">sworn in for a second term</a> in 2013. During this term, he promoted inclusiveness for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LGBT_American" title="LGBT American">LGBT Americans</a>. His administration filed briefs that urged the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> to strike down <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Same-sex marriage in the United States">same-sex marriage</a> bans as unconstitutional (<i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Windsor" title="United States v. Windsor">United States v. Windsor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges" title="Obergefell v. Hodges">Obergefell v. Hodges</a></i>); same-sex marriage was fully <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Same-sex marriage in the United States">legalized</a> in 2015 after the Court ruled that a same-sex marriage ban was unconstitutional in <i><a href="/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges" title="Obergefell v. Hodges">Obergefell</a></i>. He advocated for <a href="/wiki/Gun_politics_in_the_United_States" title="Gun politics in the United States">gun control</a> in response to the <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook_Elementary_School_shooting" title="Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting">Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting</a>, indicating support for a ban on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assault_weapons" title="Assault weapons">assault weapons</a>, and issued wide-ranging executive actions concerning <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and immigration. In foreign policy, he ordered <a href="/wiki/American-led_intervention_in_Iraq_(2014%E2%80%93present)" title="American-led intervention in Iraq (2014–present)">military intervention in Iraq</a> in response to gains made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISIL" title="ISIL">ISIL</a> after the 2011 withdrawal from Iraq, continued the process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ending_U.S._combat_operations_in_Afghanistan" title="Ending U.S. combat operations in Afghanistan">ending U.S. combat operations in Afghanistan</a> in 2016, promoted discussions that led to the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> on global climate change, initiated <a href="/wiki/International_sanctions_during_the_Ukrainian_crisis" title="International sanctions during the Ukrainian crisis">sanctions against Russia</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine_(2014%E2%80%93present)" title="Russian military intervention in Ukraine (2014–present)">invasion in Ukraine</a> and again after <a href="/wiki/Russian_interference_in_the_2016_United_States_elections" title="Russian interference in the 2016 United States elections">Russian interference in the 2016 United States elections</a>, brokered a <a href="/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of_Action" title="Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action">nuclear deal with Iran</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93Cuban_Thaw" title="United States–Cuban Thaw">normalized U.S. relations with Cuba</a>. Obama nominated <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_Supreme_Court_candidates" title="Barack Obama Supreme Court candidates">three justices to the Supreme Court</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sonia_Sotomayor" title="Sonia Sotomayor">Sonia Sotomayor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan" title="Elena Kagan">Elena Kagan</a> were confirmed as justices, while <a href="/wiki/Merrick_Garland" title="Merrick Garland">Merrick Garland</a> faced unprecedented partisan <a href="/wiki/Merrick_Garland_Supreme_Court_nomination" title="Merrick Garland Supreme Court nomination">obstruction</a> and was ultimately not confirmed. During his term in office, America's <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">soft power</a> and reputation abroad significantly improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama's presidency has generally been regarded favorably, and evaluations of his presidency among historians, political scientists, and the general public place him among <a href="/wiki/Historical_rankings_of_presidents_of_the_United_States#Notable_scholar_surveys" title="Historical rankings of presidents of the United States">the upper tier</a> of American presidents. Obama left office and retired in January 2017 and currently resides in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_Does_Trump_Stack_Up_Against_the_Best—and_Worst—Presidents?_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-How_Does_Trump_Stack_Up_Against_the_Best—and_Worst—Presidents?-4">[4]</a></sup> A December 2018 <a href="/wiki/Gallup%27s_most_admired_man_and_woman_poll" title="Gallup's most admired man and woman poll">Gallup poll</a> found Obama to be the most admired man in America for an unprecedented 11th consecutive year, although <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> was selected most admired in twelve non-consecutive years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>

%&|<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span></h2>
<p>Obama was born on August 4, 1961,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-biography-6">[6]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Kapiolani_Medical_Center_for_Women_and_Children" title="Kapiolani Medical Center for Women and Children">Kapiolani Medical Center for Women and Children</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, Hawaii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birth-certificate_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-birth-certificate-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maraniss_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-maraniss-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He is the only president born outside the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous</a> 48 states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarretoO'Bryant2013_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BarretoO'Bryant2013-10">[10]</a></sup> He was born to a white mother and a black father. His mother, <a href="/wiki/Ann_Dunham" title="Ann Dunham">Ann Dunham</a> (1942–1995), was born in <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita, Kansas</a>; she was mostly of English descent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with some German, Irish, Scottish, Swiss, and Welsh ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His father, <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_Sr." title="Barack Obama Sr.">Barack Obama Sr.</a> (1936–1982), was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luo_people_of_Kenya_and_Tanzania" title="Luo people of Kenya and Tanzania">Luo Kenyan</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nyang%27oma_Kogelo" title="Nyang'oma Kogelo">Nyang'oma Kogelo</a>. Obama's parents met in 1960 in a <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian language</a> class at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii_at_Manoa" title="University of Hawaii at Manoa">University of Hawaii at Manoa</a>, where his father was a foreign student on a scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2007-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obama_1995,_2004,_pp._9–10_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obama_1995,_2004,_pp._9–10-14">[14]</a></sup> The couple married in <a href="/wiki/Wailuku,_Hawaii" title="Wailuku, Hawaii">Wailuku, Hawaii</a>, on February 2, 1961, six months before Obama was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ripley_2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley_2008-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In late August 1961, a few weeks after he was born, Barack and his mother moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, where they lived for a year. During that time, the elder Obama completed his undergraduate degree in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> in Hawaii, graduating in June 1962. He left to attend graduate school on a scholarship at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, where he earned an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">M.A.</a> in economics. Obama's parents divorced in March 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Obama Sr. returned to Kenya in 1964, where he married for a third time and worked for the Kenyan government as the Senior Economic Analyst in the Ministry of Finance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He visited his son in Hawaii only once, at Christmas 1971,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> before he was killed in an automobile accident in 1982, when Obama was 21 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Recalling his early childhood, Obama said, "That my father looked nothing like the people around me—that he was black as pitch, my mother white as milk—barely registered in my mind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obama_1995,_2004,_pp._9–10_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Obama_1995,_2004,_pp._9–10-14">[14]</a></sup> He described his struggles as a young adult to reconcile social perceptions of his multiracial heritage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
<p>In 1963, Dunham met <a href="/wiki/Lolo_Soetoro" title="Lolo Soetoro">Lolo Soetoro</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a>; he was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_Indonesian" title="Native Indonesian">Indonesian</a> <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Center" title="East–West Center">East–West Center</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graduate_student" title="Graduate student">graduate student</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>. The couple married on <a href="/wiki/Molokai" title="Molokai">Molokai</a> on March 15, 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> After two one-year extensions of his <a href="/wiki/J-1_visa" title="J-1 visa">J-1 visa</a>, Lolo returned to <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> in 1966. His wife and stepson followed sixteen months later in 1967. The family initially lived in the Menteng Dalam neighborhood in the <a href="/wiki/Tebet,_South_Jakarta" title="Tebet, South Jakarta">Tebet</a> sub district of <a href="/wiki/South_Jakarta" title="South Jakarta">south Jakarta</a>. From 1970, they lived in a wealthier neighborhood in the <a href="/wiki/Menteng" title="Menteng">Menteng</a> sub district of <a href="/wiki/Central_Jakarta" title="Central Jakarta">central Jakarta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
<p>Obama started in St. Francis Pre-Education from age three to five. From age six to ten, he then attended local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_Language" title="Indonesian Language">Indonesian-language</a> schools: <i>Sekolah Dasar Katolik Santo Fransiskus Asisi</i> (St. Francis of Assisi Catholic Elementary School) for two years and <i><a href="/wiki/State_Elementary_School_Menteng_01" title="State Elementary School Menteng 01">Sekolah Dasar Negeri Menteng 01</a></i> (State Elementary School Menteng 01/Besuki school) for one and a half years, supplemented by English-language <a href="/wiki/Calvert_School" title="Calvert School">Calvert School</a> homeschooling by his mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> As a result of those four years in <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, he was able to speak <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> fluently as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-in_Jakarta_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-in_Jakarta-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-in_Jakarta2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-in_Jakarta2-28">[28]</a></sup> During his time in Indonesia, Obama's step-father taught him to be resilient and gave him "a pretty hardheaded assessment of how the world works".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1971, Obama returned to <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> to live with his maternal grandparents, <a href="/wiki/Madelyn_Dunham" title="Madelyn Dunham">Madelyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Armour_Dunham" title="Stanley Armour Dunham">Stanley Dunham</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/Punahou_School" title="Punahou School">Punahou School</a>—a private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University-preparatory_school" title="University-preparatory school">college preparatory school</a>—with the aid of a scholarship from fifth grade until he graduated from high school in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In his youth, Obama went by the nickname "Barry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Obama lived with his mother and half-sister, <a href="/wiki/Maya_Soetoro-Ng" title="Maya Soetoro-Ng">Maya Soetoro</a>, in Hawaii for three years from 1972 to 1975 while his mother was a graduate student in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Obama chose to stay in Hawaii with his grandparents for high school at <a href="/wiki/Punahou_School" title="Punahou School">Punahou</a> when his mother and half-sister returned to Indonesia in 1975 so his mother could begin anthropology field work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> His mother spent most of the next two decades in Indonesia, divorcing Lolo in 1980 and earning a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> degree in 1992, before dying in 1995 in Hawaii following unsuccessful treatment for <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uterine_cancer" title="Uterine cancer">uterine cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama later reflected on his years in Honolulu and wrote: "The opportunity that Hawaii offered—to experience a variety of cultures in a climate of mutual respect—became an integral part of my world view, and a basis for the values that I hold most dear."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Obama has also written and talked about using <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> during his teenage years to "push questions of who I was out of my mind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Obama was also a member of the "choom gang", a self-named group of friends who spent time together and occasionally smoked marijuana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>After graduating from high school in 1979, Obama moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to attend <a href="/wiki/Occidental_College" title="Occidental College">Occidental College</a>. In February 1981, Obama made his first public speech, calling for Occidental to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment_from_South_Africa" title="Disinvestment from South Africa">disinvestment from South Africa</a> in response to that nation's policy of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Occidental_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Occidental-39">[39]</a></sup> In mid-1981, Obama traveled to Indonesia to visit his mother and half-sister Maya, and visited the families of college friends in <a <p>Obama is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> Christian whose religious views developed in his adult life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christian_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christian-84">[84]</a></sup> He wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Audacity_of_Hope" title="The Audacity of Hope">The Audacity of Hope</a></i> that he "was not raised in a religious household". He described his father as a "confirmed <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>" by the time his parents met, and his stepfather as "a man who saw religion as not particularly useful". Obama explained how, through working with <a href="/wiki/Black_church" title="Black church">black churches</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Community_organizing" title="Community organizing">community organizer</a> while in his twenties, he came to understand "the power of the African-American religious tradition to spur social change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-social_change_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-social_change-85">[85]</a></sup>

<p>Despite being offered a full scholarship to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwestern_University_School_of_Law" title="Northwestern University School of Law">Northwestern University School of Law</a>, Obama enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> in the fall of 1988, living in nearby <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> He was selected as an editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_Review" title="Harvard Law Review">Harvard Law Review</a></i> at the end of his first year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_Law_2007_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_Law_2007-101">[101]</a></sup> president of the journal in his second year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_Law_1990a_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_Law_1990a-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_Law_1990b_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_Law_1990b-102">[102]</a></sup> and research assistant to the constitutional scholar <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Tribe" title="Laurence Tribe">Laurence Tribe</a> while at Harvard for two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> During his summers, he returned to Chicago, where he worked as a <a href="/wiki/Associate_attorney#Summer_associates" title="Associate attorney">summer associate</a> at the law firms of <a href="/wiki/Sidley_Austin" title="Sidley Austin">Sidley Austin</a> in 1989 and <a href="/wiki/Hopkins_%26_Sutter" title="Hopkins &amp; Ill.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class-action_lawsuit" title="Class-action lawsuit">class action lawsuit</a> was filed in 1994 with Selma Buycks-Roberson as lead plaintiff and alleged that Citibank Federal Savings Bank had engaged in practices forbidden under the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Credit_Opportunity_Act" title="Equal Credit Opportunity Act">Equal Credit Opportunity Act</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fair_Housing_Act" title="Fair Housing Act">Fair Housing Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> The case was settled out of court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Final judgment was issued on May 13, 1998, with Citibank Federal Savings Bank agreeing to pay attorney fees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> His law license became inactive in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavisMiner_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-DavisMiner-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1994 to 2002, Obama served on the boards of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Woods_Fund_of_Chicago" title="Woods Fund of Chicago">Woods Fund of Chicago</a>—which in 1985 had been the first foundation to fund the Developing Communities Project—and of the <a href="/wiki/Joyce_Foundation" title="Joyce Foundation">Joyce Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who's_Who_2008_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Who's_Who_2008-46">[46]</a></sup> He served on the board of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Annenberg_Challenge" title="Chicago Annenberg Challenge">Chicago Annenberg Challenge</a> from 1995 to 2002, as founding president and chairman of the board of directors from 1995 to 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who's_Who_2008_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-Who's_Who_2008-46">[46]</a></sup>
<p>Obama was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Senate" title="Illinois Senate">Illinois Senate</a> in 1996, succeeding Democratic State Senator <a href="/wiki/Alice_Palmer_(politician)" title="Alice Palmer (politician)">Alice Palmer</a> from Illinois's 13th District, which, at that time, spanned Chicago South Side neighborhoods from <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Chicago" title="Hyde Park, Chicago">Hyde Park</a>–<a href="/wiki/Kenwood,_Chicago" title="Kenwood, Chicago">Kenwood</a> south to <a href="/wiki/South_Shore,_Chicago" title="South Shore, Chicago">South Shore</a> and west to <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Lawn,_Chicago" title="Chicago Lawn, Chicago">Chicago Lawn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Once elected, Obama gained bipartisan support for legislation that reformed ethics and health care laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> He sponsored a law that increased <a href="/wiki/Tax_credit" title="Tax credit">tax credits</a> for low-income workers, negotiated <a href="/wiki/Welfare_reform" title="Welfare reform">welfare reform</a>, and promoted increased subsidies for childcare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott20070730_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott20070730-118">[118]</a></sup> In 2001, as co-chairman of the bipartisan Joint Committee on Administrative Rules, Obama supported Republican Governor Ryan's <a href="/wiki/Payday_loan" title="Payday loan">payday loan</a> regulations and <a href="/wiki/Predatory_lending" title="Predatory lending">predatory mortgage lending</a> regulations aimed at averting home <a href="/wiki/Foreclosure" title="Foreclosure">foreclosures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was reelected to the Illinois Senate in 1998, defeating Republican Yesse Yehudah in the general election, and was re-elected again in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> In 2000, he lost a <a href="/wiki/2000_Illinois%27s_1st_congressional_district_election" title="2000 Illinois's 1st congressional district election">Democratic primary race</a> for <a href="/wiki/Illinois%27s_1st_congressional_district" title="Illinois's 1st congressional district">Illinois's 1st congressional district</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> to four-term incumbent <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Rush" title="Bobby Rush">Bobby Rush</a> by a margin of two to one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Democratic_primary_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-Democratic_primary-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2003, Obama became chairman of the Illinois Senate's Health and Human Services Committee when Democrats, after a decade in the minority, regained a majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> He sponsored and led unanimous, bipartisan passage of legislation to monitor <a href="/wiki/Racial_profiling" title="Racial profiling">racial profiling</a> by requiring police to record the race of drivers they detained, and legislation making Illinois the first state to mandate videotaping of homicide interrogations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott20070730_118-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott20070730-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> During his 2004 general election campaign for the U.S. Senate, police representatives credited Obama for his active engagement with police organizations in enacting <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States" title="Capital punishment in the United States">death penalty</a> reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> Obama resigned from the Illinois Senate in November 2004 following his election to the U.S. Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
<p>In May 2002, Obama commissioned a poll to assess his prospects in a 2004 U.S. Senate race. He created a campaign committee, began raising funds, and lined up political media consultant <a href="/wiki/David_Axelrod_(political_consultant)" title="David Axelrod (political consultant)">David Axelrod</a> by August 2002. Obama formally announced his candidacy in January 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama was an early opponent of the <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> administration's <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> On October 2, 2002, the day President Bush and Congress agreed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_Resolution" title="Iraq Resolution">joint resolution</a> authorizing the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rose_Garden_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rose_Garden-128">[128]</a></sup> Obama addressed the first high-profile Chicago <a href="/wiki/Protests_against_the_Iraq_War" title="Protests against the Iraq War">anti-Iraq War rally</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Federal_Plaza_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Federal_Plaza-129">[129]</a></sup> and spoke out against the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spoke_out_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-spoke_out-130">[130]</a></sup> He addressed another anti-war rally in March 2003 and told the crowd "it's not too late" to stop the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stop_the_war_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-stop_the_war-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p><p>Decisions by Republican incumbent <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fitzgerald_(politician)" title="Peter Fitzgerald (politician)">Peter Fitzgerald</a> and his Democratic predecessor <a href="/wiki/Carol_Moseley_Braun" title="Carol Moseley Braun">Carol Moseley Braun</a> to not participate in the election resulted in wide-open Democratic and Republican primary contests involving 15 candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> In the March 2004 primary election, Obama won in an unexpected landslide—which overnight made him a rising star within the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_National_Committee" title="Democratic National Committee">national Democratic Party</a>, started speculation about a presidential future, and led to the reissue of his memoir, <i>Dreams from My Father</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-future_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-future-133">[133]</a></sup> In July 2004, Obama delivered the keynote address at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2004 Democratic National Convention">2004 Democratic National Convention</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> seen by nine million viewers. His speech was well received and elevated his status within the Democratic Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-status-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama's expected opponent in the general election, Republican primary winner <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ryan_(politician)" title="Jack Ryan (politician)">Jack Ryan</a>, withdrew from the race in June 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup> Six weeks later, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Keyes" title="Alan Keyes">Alan Keyes</a> accepted the Republican nomination to replace Ryan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Illinois,_2004" title="United States Senate election in Illinois, 2004">November 2004 general election</a>, Obama won with 70% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-margin_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-margin-138">[138]</a></sup>
<p>Obama was sworn in as a senator on January 3, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> becoming the only Senate member of the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Black_Caucus" title="Congressional Black Caucus">Congressional Black Caucus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Congressional_Quarterly" title="Congressional Quarterly">CQ Weekly</a></i> characterized him as a "loyal Democrat" based on analysis of all Senate votes from 2005 to 2007. Obama announced on November 13, 2008, that he would <a href="/wiki/Resignation_from_the_United_States_Senate" title="Resignation from the United States Senate">resign his Senate seat</a> on November 16, 2008, before the start of the <a href="/wiki/Lame_duck_(politics)" title="Lame duck (politics)">lame-duck</a> session, to focus on his transition period for the presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transition_period_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-transition_period-141">[141]</a></sup>
<p>Obama <a href="/wiki/Sponsor_(legislative)" title="Sponsor (legislative)">cosponsored</a> the <a href="/wiki/Secure_America_and_Orderly_Immigration_Act" title="Secure America and Orderly Immigration Act">Secure America and Orderly Immigration Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> He introduced two initiatives that bore his name: Lugar–Obama, which expanded the <a href="/wiki/Nunn%E2%80%93Lugar_Cooperative_Threat_Reduction" title="Nunn–Lugar Cooperative Threat Reduction">Nunn–Lugar Cooperative Threat Reduction</a> concept to conventional weapons;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Funding_Accountability_and_Transparency_Act_of_2006" title="Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act of 2006">Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act of 2006</a>, which authorized the establishment of USAspending.gov, a web search engine on federal spending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> On June 3, 2008, Senator Obama—along with Senators <a href="/wiki/Tom_Carper" title="Tom Carper">Tom Carper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Coburn" title="Tom Coburn">Tom Coburn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a>—introduced follow-up legislation: Strengthening Transparency and Accountability in Federal Spending Act of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama sponsored legislation that would have required nuclear plant owners to notify state and local authorities of radioactive leaks, but the bill failed to pass in the full Senate after being heavily modified in committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> Regarding <a href="/wiki/Tort_reform" title="Tort reform">tort reform</a>, Obama voted for the <a href="/wiki/Class_Action_Fairness_Act_of_2005" title="Class Action Fairness Act of 2005">Class Action Fairness Act of 2005</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Intelligence_Surveillance_Act_of_1978_Amendments_Act_of_2008" title="Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 Amendments Act of 2008">FISA Amendments Act of 2008</a>, which grants immunity from civil liability to telecommunications companies complicit with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSA_warrantless_surveillance_(2001%E2%80%9307)" title="NSA warrantless surveillance (2001–07)">NSA warrantless wiretapping</a> operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fisher_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-147">[147]</a></sup>
<p>In December 2006, President Bush signed into law the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> Relief, Security, and Democracy Promotion Act, marking the first federal legislation to be enacted with Obama as its primary sponsor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> In January 2007, Obama and Senator Feingold introduced a corporate jet provision to the <a href="/wiki/Honest_Leadership_and_Open_Government_Act" title="Honest Leadership and Open Government Act">Honest Leadership and Open Government Act</a>, which was signed into law in September 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> Obama also introduced two unsuccessful bills: the <a href="/wiki/Deceptive_Practices_and_Voter_Intimidation_Prevention_Act" title="Deceptive Practices and Voter Intimidation Prevention Act">Deceptive Practices and Voter Intimidation Prevention Act</a> to criminalize deceptive practices in federal elections,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War_De-Escalation_Act_of_2007" title="Iraq War De-Escalation Act of 2007">Iraq War De-Escalation Act of 2007</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup>
</p><p>Later in 2007, Obama sponsored an amendment to the Defense Authorization Act to add safeguards for personality-disorder military discharges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> This amendment passed the full Senate in the spring of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> He sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment_from_Iran" title="Disinvestment from Iran">Iran Sanctions Enabling Act</a> supporting divestment of state pension funds from Iran's oil and gas industry, which was never enacted but later incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Iran_Sanctions,_Accountability,_and_Divestment_Act_of_2010" title="Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act of 2010">Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act of 2010</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> and co-sponsored legislation to reduce risks of nuclear terrorism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuclear_terrorism_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuclear_terrorism-156">[156]</a></sup> Obama also sponsored a Senate amendment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Children%27s_Health_Insurance_Program" title="State Children's Health Insurance Program">State Children's Health Insurance Program</a>, providing one year of job protection for family members caring for soldiers with combat-related injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup>
<p>Obama held assignments on the Senate Committees for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Foreign_Relations" title="United States Senate Committee on Foreign Relations">Foreign Relations</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Environment_and_Public_Works" title="United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works">Environment and Public Works</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Veterans%27_Affairs" title="United States Senate Committee on Veterans' Affairs">Veterans' Affairs</a> through December 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup> In January 2007, he left the Environment and Public Works committee and took additional assignments with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Health,_Education,_Labor_and_Pensions" title="United States Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor and Pensions">Health, Education, Labor and Pensions</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Homeland_Security_and_Governmental_Affairs" title="United States Senate Committee on Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs">Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> He also became Chairman of the Senate's subcommittee on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Foreign_Relations_Subcommittee_on_Europe_and_Regional_Security_Cooperation" title="United States Senate Foreign Relations Subcommittee on Europe and Regional Security Cooperation">European Affairs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> As a member of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, Obama made official trips to Eastern Europe, the Middle East, Central Asia and Africa. He met with <a href="/wiki/Mahmoud_Abbas" title="Mahmoud Abbas">Mahmoud Abbas</a> before Abbas became <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Palestinian_National_Authority" title="President of the Palestinian National Authority">President of the Palestinian National Authority</a>, and gave a speech at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nairobi" title="University of Nairobi">University of Nairobi</a> in which he condemned corruption within the Kenyan government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenyan_161-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenyan-161">[161]</a></sup>
%&|<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_campaigns">Presidential campaigns</span></h2>
<p>On February 10, 2007, Obama announced his candidacy for President of the United States in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_State_Capitol_State_Historic_Site_(Illinois)" title="Old State Capitol State Historic Site (Illinois)">Old State Capitol</a> building in <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_Illinois" title="Springfield, Illinois">Springfield, Illinois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChicagoTribune_Pearson_20070210_162-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoTribune_Pearson_20070210-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC20070210_163-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20070210-163">[163]</a></sup> The choice of the announcement site was viewed as symbolic because it was also where <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> delivered his historic <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_House_Divided_Speech" title="Lincoln's House Divided Speech">"House Divided"</a> speech in 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChicagoTribune_Pearson_20070210_162-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoTribune_Pearson_20070210-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChicagoTribune_Parsons20070210_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoTribune_Parsons20070210-164">[164]</a></sup> Obama emphasized issues of rapidly ending the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, increasing <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Energy policy of the United States">energy independence</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_reform_in_the_United_States" title="Health care reform in the United States">reforming the health care system</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup> in a campaign that projected themes of hope and change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous candidates entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_presidential_primaries,_2008" title="Democratic Party presidential primaries, 2008">Democratic Party presidential primaries</a>. The field narrowed to a duel between Obama and Senator <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> after early contests, with the race remaining close throughout the primary process but with Obama gaining a steady lead in pledged <a href="/wiki/Delegate_(American_politics)" title="Delegate (American politics)">delegates</a> due to better long-range planning, superior fundraising, dominant organizing in <a href="/wiki/Caucus" title="Caucus">caucus</a> states, and better exploitation of delegate allocation rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allocation_167-0"><a href="#cite_note-allocation-167">[167]</a></sup> On June 7, 2008, Clinton ended her campaign and endorsed Obama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup>
<p>On August 23, Obama announced his selection of <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> as his vice presidential running mate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagourney_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-nagourney-169">[169]</a></sup> Obama selected Biden from a field speculated to include former Indiana Governor and Senator <a href="/wiki/Evan_Bayh" title="Evan Bayh">Evan Bayh</a> and Virginia Governor <a href="/wiki/Tim_Kaine" title="Tim Kaine">Tim Kaine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagourney_169-1"><a href="#cite_note-nagourney-169">[169]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/2008_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2008 Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Convention</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, Colorado, Hillary Clinton called for her supporters to endorse Obama, and she and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> gave convention speeches in his support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delegates_170-0"><a href="#cite_note-delegates-170">[170]</a></sup> Obama delivered his acceptance speech, not at the center where the Democratic National Convention was held, but at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invesco_Field_at_Mile_High" title="Invesco Field at Mile High">Invesco Field at Mile High</a> to a crowd of approximately 84,000 people; the speech was viewed by over 38 million people worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr1409_171-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr1409-171">[171]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acceptance_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-acceptance-172">[172]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup>
</p><p>During both the primary process and the general election, Obama's campaign set numerous fundraising records, particularly in the quantity of small donations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-small_donations_174-0"><a href="#cite_note-small_donations-174">[174]</a></sup> On June 19, 2008, Obama became the first major-party presidential candidate to turn down <a href="/wiki/Campaign_finance_in_the_United_States#Public_financing_of_campaigns" title="Campaign finance in the United States">public financing</a> in the general election since the system was created in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Salant_20080619_175-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Salant_20080619-175">[175]</a></sup>
<p>John McCain was nominated as the Republican candidate, and he selected <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Palin" title="Sarah Palin">Sarah Palin</a> as his running mate. The two candidates engaged in three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election_debates" title="United States presidential election debates">presidential debates</a> in September and October 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presidential_debates_176-0"><a href="#cite_note-presidential_debates-176">[176]</a></sup> On November 4, Obama won the presidency with 365 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" title="Electoral College (United States)">electoral votes</a> to 173 received by McCain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electoral_votes_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-electoral_votes-177">[177]</a></sup> Obama won 52.9% of the <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">popular vote</a> to McCain's 45.7%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup> He became the first African American to be elected president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup> Obama delivered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_election_victory_speech,_2008" title="Barack Obama election victory speech, 2008">his victory speech</a> before hundreds of thousands of supporters in Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Grant_Park_(Chicago)" title="Grant Park (Chicago)">Grant Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent1_180-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent1-180">[180]</a></sup>
%&|<p>On April 4, 2011, Obama announced his reelection campaign for 2012 in a video titled "It Begins with Us" that he posted on his website and filed election papers with the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Election_Commission" title="Federal Election Commission">Federal Election Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup> As the incumbent president he ran virtually unopposed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_presidential_primaries,_2012" title="Democratic Party presidential primaries, 2012">Democratic Party presidential primaries</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN-clinch1_184-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-clinch1-184">[184]</a></sup> and on April 3, 2012, Obama had secured the 2778 <a href="/wiki/2012_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2012 Democratic National Convention">convention</a> delegates needed to win the Democratic nomination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN-clinch2_185-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-clinch2-185">[185]</a></sup>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2012_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2012 Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Convention</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>, Obama and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> were formally nominated by former President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> as the Democratic Party candidates for president and vice president in the general election. Their main opponents were Republicans <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a>, the former governor of Massachusetts, and Representative <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ryan" title="Paul Ryan">Paul Ryan</a> of Wisconsin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 6, 2012, Obama won 332 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" title="Electoral College (United States)">electoral votes</a>, exceeding the 270 required for him to be reelected as president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup> With 51.1% of the popular vote,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FEC_190-0"><a href="#cite_note-FEC-190">[190]</a></sup> Obama became the first Democratic president since <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> to win the <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_presidential_elections_by_popular_vote_margin" title="List of United States presidential elections by popular vote margin">majority of the popular vote</a> twice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup> Obama addressed supporters and volunteers at Chicago's <a href="/wiki/McCormick_Place" title="McCormick Place">McCormick Place</a> after his reelection and said: "Tonight you voted for action, not politics as usual. You elected us to focus on your jobs, not ours. And in the coming weeks and months, I am looking forward to reaching out and working with leaders of both parties."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20130121_194-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20130121-194">[194]</a></sup>
%&|<h2><span id="Presidency_.282009.E2.80.932017.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency_(2009–2017)">Presidency (2009–2017)</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="First inauguration of Barack Obama">inauguration of Barack Obama</a> as the 44th President took place on January 20, 2009. In his first few days in office, Obama issued <a href="/wiki/Executive_order" title="Executive order">executive orders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Presidential_memorandum" title="Presidential memorandum">presidential memoranda</a> directing the U.S. military to develop plans to withdraw troops from Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup> He ordered the closing of the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detention_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detention camp">Guantanamo Bay detention camp</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup> but Congress prevented the closure by refusing to appropriate the required funds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup> and preventing moving any Guantanamo detainee into the U.S. or to other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup> Obama reduced the secrecy given to presidential records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201"><a href="#cite_note-201">[201]</a></sup> He also revoked President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s restoration of President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexico_City_Policy" title="Mexico City Policy">Mexico City Policy</a> prohibiting federal aid to international family planning organizations that perform or provide counseling about abortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-202"><a href="#cite_note-202">[202]</a></sup>
<p>The first bill signed into law by Obama was the <a href="/wiki/Lilly_Ledbetter_Fair_Pay_Act_of_2009" title="Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009">Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009</a>, relaxing the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_limitations" title="Statute of limitations">statute of limitations</a> for equal-pay lawsuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup> Five days later, he signed the reauthorization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Children%27s_Health_Insurance_Program" title="State Children's Health Insurance Program">State Children's Health Insurance Program</a> (SCHIP) to cover an additional 4 million uninsured children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-204"><a href="#cite_note-204">[204]</a></sup> In March 2009, Obama reversed a Bush-era policy that had limited funding of <a href="/wiki/Embryonic_stem_cell" title="Embryonic stem cell">embryonic stem cell</a> research and pledged to develop "strict guidelines" on the research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-205"><a href="#cite_note-205">[205]</a></sup>
<p>Obama appointed two women to serve on the Supreme Court in the first two years of his Presidency. He nominated <a href="/wiki/Sonia_Sotomayor" title="Sonia Sotomayor">Sonia Sotomayor</a> on May 26, 2009 to replace retiring <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">Associate Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Souter" title="David Souter">David Souter</a>; she was confirmed on August 6, 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup> becoming the first Supreme Court Justice of <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[207]</a></sup> Obama nominated <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan" title="Elena Kagan">Elena Kagan</a> on May 10, 2010 to replace retiring Associate Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a>. She was confirmed on August 5, 2010, bringing the number of women sitting simultaneously on the Court to three justices for the first time in American history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-208"><a href="#cite_note-208">[208]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 30, 2010, Obama signed the <a href="/wiki/Health_Care_and_Education_Reconciliation_Act_of_2010" title="Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act of 2010">Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Reconciliation_(United_States_Congress)" title="Reconciliation (United States Congress)">reconciliation bill</a> that ended the process of the federal government giving subsidies to private banks to give out federally insured loans, increased the <a href="/wiki/Pell_Grant" title="Pell Grant">Pell Grant</a> scholarship award, and made changes to the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-209"><a href="#cite_note-209">[209]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-210"><a href="#cite_note-210">[210]</a></sup>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Barack_Obama_administration" title="Space policy of the Barack Obama administration">major space policy speech</a> in April 2010, Obama announced a planned change in direction at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, the U.S. space agency. He ended plans for a return of <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> to the moon and development of the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> rocket, <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a> rocket and <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a>, in favor of funding Earth science projects, a new rocket type, and research and development for an eventual manned mission to Mars, and ongoing missions to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-211"><a href="#cite_note-211">[211]</a></sup>
</p><p>President Obama's <a href="/wiki/2011_State_of_the_Union_Address" title="2011 State of the Union Address">2011 State of the Union Address</a> focused on themes of education and innovation, stressing the importance of <a href="/wiki/Innovation_economics" title="Innovation economics">innovation economics</a> to make the United States more competitive globally. He spoke of a five-year freeze in domestic spending, eliminating tax breaks for oil companies and reversing tax cuts for the wealthiest Americans, banning congressional <a href="/wiki/Earmark_(politics)" title="Earmark (politics)">earmarks</a>, and reducing healthcare costs. He promised the United States would have one million electric vehicles on the road by 2015 and be 80% reliant on "<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">clean</a>" electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-212"><a href="#cite_note-212">[212]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-213"><a href="#cite_note-213">[213]</a></sup>
<p>On October 8, 2009, Obama signed the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Shepard_and_James_Byrd_Jr._Hate_Crimes_Prevention_Act" title="Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act">Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act</a>, a measure that expanded the <a href="/wiki/Hate_crime_laws_in_the_United_States#Federal_prosecution_of_hate_crimes" title="Hate crime laws in the United States">1969 United States federal hate-crime law</a> to include crimes motivated by a victim's actual or perceived gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-214"><a href="#cite_note-214">[214]</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 30, 2009, Obama lifted the ban on travel to the United States by those infected with <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>, which was celebrated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigration_Equality" title="Immigration Equality">Immigration Equality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-215"><a href="#cite_note-215">[215]</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 22, 2010, Obama signed the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Ask,_Don%27t_Tell_Repeal_Act_of_2010" title="Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010">Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010</a>, which fulfilled a key promise made in the 2008 presidential campaign<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-216"><a href="#cite_note-216">[216]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-217"><a href="#cite_note-217">[217]</a></sup> to end the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">Don't ask, don't tell</a> policy of 1993 that had prevented gay and lesbian people from serving openly in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-218"><a href="#cite_note-218">[218]</a></sup> In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a> also ended the policy that barred <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> people from serving openly in the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TransBan1_219-0"><a href="#cite_note-TransBan1-219">[219]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a candidate for the Illinois state senate in 1996, Obama had said he favored legalizing <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Same-sex marriage in the United States">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCT0114_220-0"><a href="#cite_note-WCT0114-220">[220]</a></sup> By the time of his Senate run in 2004, he said he supported civil unions and domestic partnerships for same-sex partners but opposed same-sex marriages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCT0204_221-0"><a href="#cite_note-WCT0204-221">[221]</a></sup> In 2008, he reaffirmed this position by stating "I believe marriage is between a man and a woman. I am not in favor of gay marriage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-222"><a href="#cite_note-222">[222]</a></sup> On May 9, 2012, shortly after the official launch of his campaign for re-election as president, Obama said his views had evolved, and he publicly affirmed his personal support for the legalization of same-sex marriage, becoming the first sitting U.S. president to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-223"><a href="#cite_note-223">[223]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Huffington_Post_224-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Huffington_Post-224">[224]</a></sup>
<p>During his second <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="Second inauguration of Barack Obama">inaugural address</a> on January 21, 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20130121_194-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20130121-194">[194]</a></sup> Obama became the first U.S. President in office to call for full equality for gay Americans: "Our journey is not complete until our gay brothers and sisters are treated like anyone else under the law—for if we are truly created equal, then surely the love we commit to one another must be equal as well." This was the first time that a president mentioned <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_the_United_States" title="LGBT rights in the United States">gay rights</a> or the word "gay" in an inaugural address.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-225"><a href="#cite_note-225">[225]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-226"><a href="#cite_note-226">[226]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the Obama Administration filed briefs that urged the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> to rule in favor of same-sex couples in the cases of <i><a href="/wiki/Hollingsworth_v._Perry" title="Hollingsworth v. Perry">Hollingsworth v. Perry</a></i> (regarding <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Same-sex marriage in the United States">same-sex marriage</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huffpo-amicus_227-0"><a href="#cite_note-huffpo-amicus-227">[227]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Windsor" title="United States v. Windsor">United States v. Windsor</a></i> (regarding the <a href="/wiki/Defense_of_Marriage_Act" title="Defense of Marriage Act">Defense of Marriage Act</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn-amicus_228-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-amicus-228">[228]</a></sup> Then, following the Supreme Court's 2015 decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges" title="Obergefell v. Hodges">Obergefell v. Hodges</a></i> (ruling same-sex marriage to be a fundamental right), Obama asserted that, "This decision affirms what millions of Americans already believe in their hearts: When all Americans are treated as equal we are all more free."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remarksDecision_229-0"><a href="#cite_note-remarksDecision-229">[229]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 30, 2015, the White House Office of National AIDS Policy revised its strategy for addressing the disease, which included widespread testing and linkage to healthcare, which was celebrated by the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Campaign" title="Human Rights Campaign">Human Rights Campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-230"><a href="#cite_note-230">[230]</a></sup>
<p>On March 11, 2009, Obama created the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Council_on_Women_and_Girls" title="White House Council on Women and Girls">White House Council on Women and Girls</a>, which formed part of the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Office_of_Intergovernmental_Affairs" title="White House Office of Intergovernmental Affairs">Office of Intergovernmental Affairs</a>, having been established by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_13506" title="s:Executive Order 13506">13506</a> with a broad mandate to advise him on issues relating to the welfare of American women and girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Executive_231-0"><a href="#cite_note-Executive-231">[231]</a></sup> The Council was chaired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senior_Advisor_to_the_President" title="Senior Advisor to the President">Senior Advisor to the President</a> <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Jarrett" title="Valerie Jarrett">Valerie Jarrett</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexualassault_232-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sexualassault-232">[232]</a></sup> Obama also established the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Task_Force_to_Protect_Students_from_Sexual_Assault" title="White House Task Force to Protect Students from Sexual Assault">White House Task Force to Protect Students from Sexual Assault</a> through a government memorandum on January 22, 2014, with a broad mandate to advise him on issues relating to sexual assault on college and university campuses throughout the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexualassault_232-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sexualassault-232">[232]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memo_233-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memo-233">[233]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedom_234-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freedom-234">[234]</a></sup> The co-chairs of the Task Force were Vice President <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> and Jarrett.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memo_233-1"><a href="#cite_note-Memo-233">[233]</a></sup> The Task Force was a development out of the White House Council on Women and Girls and <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Office of the Vice President of the United States">Office of the Vice President of the United States</a>, and prior to that the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Violence_Against_Women_Act" title="Violence Against Women Act">Violence Against Women Act</a> first drafted by Biden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Report_235-0"><a href="#cite_note-Report-235">[235]</a></sup>
<p>On February 17, 2009, Obama signed the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>, a $787 billion <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(economics)" title="Stimulus (economics)">economic stimulus</a> package aimed at helping the economy recover from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">deepening worldwide recession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-236"><a href="#cite_note-236">[236]</a></sup> The act includes increased federal spending for health care, infrastructure, education, various tax breaks and <a href="/wiki/Tax_incentive" title="Tax incentive">incentives</a>, and direct assistance to individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-direct_assistance_237-0"><a href="#cite_note-direct_assistance-237">[237]</a></sup>
<p>In March, Obama's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_Secretary" title="Treasury Secretary">Treasury Secretary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Geithner" title="Timothy Geithner">Timothy Geithner</a>, took further steps to manage the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">financial crisis</a>, including introducing the <a href="/wiki/Public%E2%80%93Private_Investment_Program_for_Legacy_Assets" title="Public–Private Investment Program for Legacy Assets">Public–Private Investment Program for Legacy Assets</a>, which contains provisions for buying up to two trillion dollars in depreciated real estate assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-markets_opened_238-0"><a href="#cite_note-markets_opened-238">[238]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama intervened in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive_industry_crisis_of_2008%E2%80%9310" title="Automotive industry crisis of 2008–10">troubled automotive industry</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-239"><a href="#cite_note-239">[239]</a></sup> in March 2009, renewing loans for <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> to continue operations while reorganizing. Over the following months the White House set terms for both firms' bankruptcies, including the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Chapter_11_reorganization" title="Chrysler Chapter 11 reorganization">sale of Chrysler</a> to Italian automaker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-240"><a href="#cite_note-240">[240]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Chapter_11_reorganization" title="General Motors Chapter 11 reorganization">reorganization of GM</a> giving the U.S. government a temporary 60% equity stake in the company, with the Canadian government taking a 12% stake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-241"><a href="#cite_note-241">[241]</a></sup> In June 2009, dissatisfied with the pace of economic stimulus, Obama called on his cabinet to accelerate the investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopher_Conkey_and_Louise_Radnofsky_242-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christopher_Conkey_and_Louise_Radnofsky-242">[242]</a></sup> He signed into law the <a href="/wiki/Car_Allowance_Rebate_System" title="Car Allowance Rebate System">Car Allowance Rebate System</a>, known colloquially as "Cash for Clunkers", which temporarily boosted the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-243"><a href="#cite_note-243">[243]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-244"><a href="#cite_note-244">[244]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-245"><a href="#cite_note-245">[245]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Bush and Obama administrations authorized spending and loan guarantees from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve</a> and the Treasury Department. These guarantees totaled about $11.5 trillion, but only $3 trillion had been spent by the end of November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-246"><a href="#cite_note-246">[246]</a></sup> Obama and the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> predicted the 2010 <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_federal_budget" title="2010 United States federal budget">budget deficit</a> would be $1.5 trillion or 10.6% of the nation's <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) compared to the 2009 deficit of $1.4 trillion or 9.9% of GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-247"><a href="#cite_note-247">[247]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-248"><a href="#cite_note-248">[248]</a></sup> For 2011, the administration predicted the deficit would shrink to $1.34 trillion, and the 10-year deficit would increase to $8.53 trillion or 90% of GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-249"><a href="#cite_note-249">[249]</a></sup> The most recent increase in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_debt_ceiling" title="United States debt ceiling">debt ceiling</a> to $17.2 trillion took effect in February 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-250"><a href="#cite_note-250">[250]</a></sup> On August 2, 2011, after a lengthy congressional debate over whether to raise the nation's debt limit, Obama signed the bipartisan <a href="/wiki/Budget_Control_Act_of_2011" title="Budget Control Act of 2011">Budget Control Act of 2011</a>. The legislation enforces limits on discretionary spending until 2021, establishes a procedure to increase the debt limit, creates a Congressional Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction to propose further deficit reduction with a stated goal of achieving at least $1.5 trillion in budgetary savings over 10 years, and establishes automatic procedures for reducing spending by as much as $1.2 trillion if legislation originating with the new joint select committee does not achieve such savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-251"><a href="#cite_note-251">[251]</a></sup> By passing the legislation, Congress was able to prevent a <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S. government</a> <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">default</a> on its obligations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-252"><a href="#cite_note-252">[252]</a></sup>
<p>As it did throughout 2008, the unemployment rate rose in 2009, reaching a peak in October at 10.0% and averaging 10.0% in the fourth quarter. Following a decrease to 9.7% in the first quarter of 2010, the unemployment rate fell to 9.6% in the second quarter, where it remained for the rest of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theodossiou_255-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theodossiou-255">[255]</a></sup> Between February and December 2010, employment rose by 0.8%, which was less than the average of 1.9% experienced during comparable periods in the past four employment recoveries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eddlemon_256-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eddlemon-256">[256]</a></sup> By November 2012, the unemployment rate fell to 7.7%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-257"><a href="#cite_note-257">[257]</a></sup> decreasing to 6.7% in the last month of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-258"><a href="#cite_note-258">[258]</a></sup> During 2014, the unemployment rate continued to decline, falling to 6.3% in the first quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-259"><a href="#cite_note-259">[259]</a></sup> GDP growth returned in the third quarter of 2009, expanding at a rate of 1.6%, followed by a 5.0% increase in the fourth quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BEA1_260-0"><a href="#cite_note-BEA1-260">[260]</a></sup> Growth continued in 2010, posting an increase of 3.7% in the first quarter, with lesser gains throughout the rest of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BEA1_260-1"><a href="#cite_note-BEA1-260">[260]</a></sup> In July 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve</a> noted that economic activity continued to increase, but its pace had slowed, and chairman <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Ben Bernanke</a> said the economic outlook was "unusually uncertain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-261"><a href="#cite_note-261">[261]</a></sup> Overall, the economy expanded at a rate of 2.9% in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-262"><a href="#cite_note-262">[262]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> (CBO) and a broad range of economists credit Obama's stimulus plan for economic growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-estimated_263-0"><a href="#cite_note-estimated-263">[263]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newc_264-0"><a </p><p>In December 2013, Obama declared that growing <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality_in_the_United_States" title="Income inequality in the United States">income inequality</a> is a "defining challenge of our time" and called on Congress to bolster the safety net and raise wages. This came on the heels of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_food_worker_strikes" title="Fast food worker strikes">nationwide strikes of fast-food workers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a>' criticism of inequality and <a href="/wiki/Trickle-down_economics" title="Trickle-down economics">trickle-down economics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-273"><a href="#cite_note-273">[273]</a></sup>

<p>On September 30, 2009, the Obama administration proposed new regulations on power plants, factories, and oil refineries in an attempt to limit greenhouse gas emissions and to curb <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-275"><a href="#cite_note-275">[275]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-276"><a href="#cite_note-276">[276]</a></sup>

Obama visited the Gulf, announced a federal investigation, and formed a bipartisan commission to recommend new safety standards, after a review by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Interior" title="United States Secretary of the Interior">Secretary of the Interior</a> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Salazar" title="Ken Salazar">Ken Salazar</a> and concurrent Congressional hearings. He then announced a six-month moratorium on new <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_drilling" title="Deepwater drilling">deepwater drilling</a> permits and leases, pending regulatory review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-277"><a href="#cite_note-277">[277]</a></sup> As multiple efforts by BP failed, some in the media and public expressed confusion and criticism over various aspects of the incident, and stated a desire for more involvement by Obama and the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-278"><a href="#cite_note-278">[278]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2013, Obama expressed reservations and said he "would reject the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keystone_XL_pipeline" title="Keystone XL pipeline">Keystone XL pipeline</a> if it increased carbon pollution" or "greenhouse emissions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-279"><a href="#cite_note-279">[279]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-280"><a href="#cite_note-280">[280]</a></sup> Obama's advisers called for a halt to <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_exploration_in_the_Arctic" title="Petroleum exploration in the Arctic">petroleum exploration in the Arctic</a> in January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-281"><a href="#cite_note-281">[281]</a></sup> On February 24, 2015, Obama vetoed a bill that would have authorized the pipeline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-282"><a href="#cite_note-282">[282]</a></sup> It was the third veto of Obama's presidency and his first major veto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-283"><a href="#cite_note-283">[283]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama emphasized the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Federal_lands" title="Federal lands">federal lands</a> during his term in office. He used his power under the <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_Act" title="Antiquities Act">Antiquities Act</a> to create 25 new <a href="/wiki/National_monument_(United_States)" title="National monument (United States)">national monuments</a> during his presidency and expand four others, protecting a total of 553,000,000 acres (224,000,000 ha) of federal lands and waters, more than any other U.S. president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monuments_284-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monuments-284">[284]</a></sup>
<p>Obama called for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> to pass legislation reforming <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">health care in the United States</a>, a key campaign promise and a top legislative goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_reform_285-0"><a href="#cite_note-health_reform-285">[285]</a></sup> He proposed an expansion of health insurance coverage to cover the uninsured, to cap premium increases, and to allow people to retain their coverage when they leave or change jobs. His proposal was to spend $900 billion over 10 years and include a government insurance plan, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Public_health_insurance_option" title="Public health insurance option">public option</a>, to compete with the corporate insurance sector as a main component to lowering costs and improving quality of health care. It would also make it illegal for insurers to drop sick people or deny them coverage for <a href="/wiki/Pre-existing_condition" title="Pre-existing condition">pre-existing conditions</a>, and require every American to carry health coverage. The plan also includes medical spending cuts and taxes on insurance companies that offer expensive plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-286"><a href="#cite_note-286">[286]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-287"><a href="#cite_note-287">[287]</a></sup>
<p>On July 14, 2009, House Democratic leaders introduced a 1,017-page plan for overhauling the U.S. health care system, which Obama wanted Congress to approve by the end of 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_reform_285-1"><a href="#cite_note-health_reform-285">[285]</a></sup> After much public debate during the Congressional summer recess of 2009, Obama delivered <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_speech_to_joint_session_of_Congress,_September_2009" title="Barack Obama speech to joint session of Congress, September 2009">a speech to a joint session of Congress</a> on September<span class="nowrap"> </span>9 where he addressed concerns over the proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-289"><a href="#cite_note-289">[289]</a></sup> In March 2009, Obama lifted a ban on using federal funds for stem cell research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-290"><a href="#cite_note-290">[290]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 7, 2009, a health care bill featuring the public option was passed in the House.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1_291-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-291">[291]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_292-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-292">[292]</a></sup> On December 24, 2009, the Senate passed its own bill—without a public option—on a party-line vote of 60–39.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-293"><a href="#cite_note-293">[293]</a></sup> On March 21, 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act</a> (ACA) passed by the Senate in December was passed in the House by a vote of 219 to 212.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-294"><a href="#cite_note-294">[294]</a></sup> Obama signed the bill into law on March 23, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-295"><a href="#cite_note-295">[295]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ACA includes <a href="/wiki/Provisions_of_the_Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Provisions of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">health-related provisions</a>, most of which took effect in 2014, including expanding <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> eligibility for people making up to 133% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_poverty_level" title="Federal poverty level">federal poverty level</a> (FPL) starting in 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_ref1_296-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn_ref1-296">[296]</a></sup> subsidizing insurance premiums for people making up to 400% of the FPL ($88,000 for family of four in 2010) so their maximum "out-of-pocket" payment for annual premiums will be from 2% to 9.5% of income,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitehouse_297-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitehouse-297">[297]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-298"><a href="#cite_note-298">[298]</a></sup> providing incentives for businesses to provide health care benefits, prohibiting denial of coverage and denial of claims based on pre-existing conditions, establishing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_insurance_exchange" title="Health insurance exchange">health insurance exchanges</a>, prohibiting annual coverage caps, and support for medical research. According to White House and CBO figures, the maximum share of income that enrollees would have to pay would vary depending on their income relative to the federal poverty level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitehouse_297-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitehouse-297">[297]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo_est_299-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbo_est-299">[299]</a></sup>
<p>The costs of these provisions are offset by taxes, fees, and cost-saving measures, such as new Medicare taxes for those in high-income <a href="/wiki/Tax_bracket" title="Tax bracket">brackets</a>, taxes on <a href="/wiki/Indoor_tanning" title="Indoor tanning">indoor tanning</a>, cuts to the <a href="/wiki/Medicare_Advantage" title="Medicare Advantage">Medicare Advantage</a> program in favor of traditional Medicare, and fees on medical devices and pharmaceutical companies;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-301"><a href="#cite_note-301">[301]</a></sup> there is also a tax penalty for those who do not obtain health insurance, unless they are exempt due to low income or other reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-302"><a href="#cite_note-302">[302]</a></sup> In March 2010, the CBO estimated that the net effect of both laws will be a reduction in the federal deficit by $143 billion over the first decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-303"><a href="#cite_note-303">[303]</a></sup>
</p><p>The law faced several legal challenges, primarily based on the argument that an individual mandate requiring Americans to buy health insurance was unconstitutional. On June 28, 2012, the Supreme Court ruled by a 5–4 vote in <i><a href="/wiki/National_Federation_of_Independent_Business_v._Sebelius" title="National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius">National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius</a></i> that the mandate was constitutional under the U.S. Congress's taxing authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP-uphold_304-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-uphold-304">[304]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Burwell_v._Hobby_Lobby_Stores,_Inc." title="Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc.">Burwell v. Hobby Lobby</a></i> the Court ruled that "closely-held" for-profit corporations could be exempt on religious grounds under the <a href="/wiki/Religious_Freedom_Restoration_Act" title="Religious Freedom Restoration Act">Religious Freedom Restoration Act</a> from regulations adopted under the ACA that would have required them to pay for insurance that covered certain contraceptives. In June 2015, the Court ruled 6–3 in <i><a href="/wiki/King_v._Burwell" title="King v. Burwell">King v. Burwell</a></i> that subsidies to help individuals and families purchase health insurance were authorized for those doing so on both the federal exchange and state exchanges, not only those purchasing plans "established by the State", as the statute reads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-305"><a href="#cite_note-305">[305]</a></sup>
<p>Prior to June 2014, Obama offered substantial support for a broadly-based "All of the above" approach to domestic <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy" title="Energy policy">energy policy</a>, which Obama has maintained since his first term and which he last confirmed at his <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union" title="State of the Union">State of the Union</a> speech in January 2014 to a mixed reception by both parties. In June 2014, Obama made indications that his administration would consider a shift towards an energy policy more closely tuned to the manufacturing industry and its impact on the domestic economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-306"><a href="#cite_note-306">[306]</a></sup> Obama's approach of selectively combining regulation and incentive to various issues in the domestic energy policy, such as coal mining and oil fracking, has received mixed commentary for not being as responsive to the needs of the domestic manufacturing sector as needed, following claims that the domestic manufacturing sector utilizes as much as a third of the nation's available energy resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-307"><a href="#cite_note-307">[307]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-308"><a href="#cite_note-308">[308]</a></sup>
<p>On January 16, 2013, one month after the <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook_Elementary_School_shooting" title="Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting">Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting</a>, Obama signed 23 executive orders and outlined a series of sweeping proposals regarding <a href="/wiki/Gun_politics_in_the_United_States" title="Gun politics in the United States">gun control</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-309"><a href="#cite_note-309">[309]</a></sup> He urged Congress to reintroduce an <a href="/wiki/Federal_Assault_Weapons_Ban" title="Federal Assault Weapons Ban">expired ban</a> on military-style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assault_weapons" title="Assault weapons">assault weapons</a>, such as those used in several recent mass shootings, impose limits on ammunition magazines to 10 rounds, introduce background checks on all gun sales, pass a ban on possession and sale of armor-piercing bullets, introduce harsher penalties for gun-traffickers, especially unlicensed dealers who buy arms for criminals and approving the appointment of the head of the federal <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives</a> for the first time since 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-310"><a href="#cite_note-310">[310]</a></sup> On January 5, 2016, Obama announced new executive actions extending background check requirements to more gun sellers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-311"><a href="#cite_note-311">[311]</a></sup> In a 2016 editorial in the <i>New York Times</i>, Obama compared the struggle for what he termed "common-sense gun reform" to <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">women's suffrage</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movements" title="Civil rights movements">civil rights movements</a> in American history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-312"><a href="#cite_note-312">[312]</a></sup>

<p>Obama spoke in front of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> in <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a>, Ethiopia, on July 29, 2015, the first sitting U.S. president to do so. He gave a speech encouraging the world to increase economic ties via investments and trade with the continent, and lauded the progress made in <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Africa" title="Education in Africa">education</a>, infrastructure, and <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Africa" title="Economy of Africa">economy</a>. He also criticized the lack of democracy and leaders who refuse to step aside, discrimination against minorities (<a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> people, religious groups and ethnicities), and corruption. He suggested an intensified <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">democratization</a> and free trade, to significantly improve the quality of life for Africans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-426"><a href="#cite_note-426">[426]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-427"><a href="#cite_note-427">[427]</a></sup> During his July 2015 trip, Obama also was the first U.S. president ever to <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_visits_to_Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="United States presidential visits to Sub-Saharan Africa">visit</a> Kenya, which is the homeland of his father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-428"><a href="#cite_note-428">[428]</a></sup>
<p>On May 27, 2016, Obama became the first sitting American president to visit <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>, Japan, 71 years after the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">U.S. atomic bombing of Hiroshima</a> that ended <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Accompanied by Japanese Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a>, Obama paid tribute to the victims of the bombing at the <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima_Peace_Memorial_Museum" title="Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum">Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-429"><a href="#cite_note-429">[429]</a></sup>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">Russia's invasion of Crimea</a> in 2014, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_military_intervention_in_Syria" title="Russian military intervention in Syria">military intervention</a> in Syria in 2015, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2016_United_States_election_interference_by_Russia" title="2016 United States election interference by Russia">interference</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election" title="2016 United States presidential election">2016 U.S. presidential election</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftelect_430-0"><a href="#cite_note-ftelect-430">[430]</a></sup> Obama's Russia policy was widely seen as a failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedmnyt_431-0"><a href="#cite_note-friedmnyt-431">[431]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Robertson,_Baron_Robertson_of_Port_Ellen" title="George Robertson, Baron Robertson of Port Ellen">George Robertson</a>, a former UK defense secretary and NATO secretary-general, said Obama had "allowed Putin to jump back on the world stage and test the resolve of the West", adding that the legacy of this disaster would last.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obexit_432-0"><a href="#cite_note-obexit-432">[432]</a></sup>
<p>Obama's family history, upbringing, and <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> education differ markedly from those of African-American politicians who launched their careers in the 1960s through participation in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-433"><a href="#cite_note-433">[433]</a></sup> Expressing puzzlement over questions about whether he is "black enough", Obama told an August 2007 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Black_Journalists" title="National Association of Black Journalists">National Association of Black Journalists</a> that "we're still locked in this notion that if you appeal to white folks then there must be something wrong."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-434"><a href="#cite_note-434">[434]</a></sup> Obama acknowledged his youthful image in an October 2007 campaign speech, saying: "I wouldn't be here if, time and again, the torch had not been passed to a new generation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-435"><a href="#cite_note-435">[435]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama is frequently referred to as an exceptional orator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exceptional_orator_436-0"><a href="#cite_note-exceptional_orator-436">[436]</a></sup> During his pre-inauguration transition period and continuing into his presidency, Obama delivered a series of weekly Internet video addresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-437"><a href="#cite_note-437">[437]</a></sup> In his speeches as president, Obama did not make more overt references to race relations than his predecessors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-438"><a href="#cite_note-438">[438]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-439"><a href="#cite_note-439">[439]</a></sup> but according to one study, he implemented stronger policy action on behalf of African-Americans than any president since the Nixon era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-440"><a href="#cite_note-440">[440]</a></sup>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallup_Organization" title="Gallup Organization">Gallup Organization</a>, Obama began his presidency with a 68% approval rating<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-441"><a href="#cite_note-441">[441]</a></sup> before gradually declining for the rest of the year, and eventually bottoming out at 41% in August 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-442"><a href="#cite_note-442">[442]</a></sup> a trend similar to <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>'s first years in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-443"><a href="#cite_note-443">[443]</a></sup> He experienced a small poll bounce shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Osama_bin_Laden" title="Death of Osama bin Laden">death of Osama bin Laden</a> on May 2, 2011. This bounce lasted until around June 2011, when his approval numbers dropped back to where they were previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-444"><a href="#cite_note-444">[444]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-445"><a href="#cite_note-445">[445]</a></sup> His approval ratings rebounded around the same time as his reelection in 2012, with polls showing an average job approval of 52% shortly after his second inauguration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-446"><a href="#cite_note-446">[446]</a></sup> Despite approval ratings dropping to 39% in late-2013 due to the ACA roll-out, they climbed to 50% in January 2015 according to Gallup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-447"><a href="#cite_note-447">[447]</a></sup>
</p><p>Polls showed strong support for Obama in other countries both before and during his presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-448"><a href="#cite_note-448">[448]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-449"><a href="#cite_note-449">[449]</a></sup> In a February 2009 poll conducted in Western Europe and the U.S. by <a href="/wiki/Harris_Insights_%26_Analytics" title="Harris Insights &amp; Analytics">Harris Interactive</a> for <a href="/wiki/France_24" title="France 24">France 24</a> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">International Herald Tribune</a></i>, Obama was rated as the most respected world leader, as well as the most powerful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-450"><a href="#cite_note-450">[450]</a></sup> In a similar poll conducted by Harris in May 2009, Obama was rated as the most popular world leader, as well as the one figure most people would pin their hopes on for pulling the world out of the economic downturn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-451"><a href="#cite_note-451">[451]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-452"><a href="#cite_note-452">[452]</a></sup>
<p>Obama won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Spoken_Word_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word Album">Best Spoken Word Album</a> <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a> for abridged <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">audiobook</a> versions of <i><a href="/wiki/Dreams_from_My_Father" title="Dreams from My Father">Dreams from My Father</a></i> in February 2006 and for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Audacity_of_Hope" title="The Audacity of Hope">The Audacity of Hope</a></i> in February 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-453"><a href="#cite_note-453">[453]</a></sup> His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_presidential_primary_campaign,_2008#New_Hampshire" title="Barack Obama presidential primary campaign, 2008">concession speech</a> after the New Hampshire primary was set to music by independent artists as the music video "<a href="/wiki/Yes_We_Can_(will.i.am_song)" title="Yes We Can (will.i.am song)">Yes We Can</a>", which was viewed 10 million times on YouTube in its first month<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-454"><a href="#cite_note-454">[454]</a></sup> and received a <a href="/wiki/Daytime_Emmy_Award" title="Daytime Emmy Award">Daytime Emmy Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-455"><a href="#cite_note-455">[455]</a></sup> In December 2008 and in 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine named Obama as its <a href="/wiki/Time_Person_of_the_Year" title="Time Person of the Year">Person of the Year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-456"><a href="#cite_note-456">[456]</a></sup> The 2008 awarding was for his historic candidacy and election, which <i>Time</i> described as "the steady march of seemingly impossible accomplishments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-457"><a href="#cite_note-457">[457]</a></sup> On May 25, 2011, Obama became the first President of the United States to address both houses of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">UK Parliament</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westminster_Hall" title="Westminster Hall">Westminster Hall</a>, London. This was only the fifth occurrence since the start of the 20th century of a head of state's being extended this invitation, following <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a> in 1960, <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> in 1996, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> in 2002 and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-458"><a href="#cite_note-458">[458]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-459"><a href="#cite_note-459">[459]</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 9, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Nobel_Committee" title="Norwegian Nobel Committee">Norwegian Nobel Committee</a> announced that Obama had won the <a href="/wiki/2009_Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="2009 Nobel Peace Prize">2009 Nobel Peace Prize</a> "for his extraordinary efforts to strengthen international diplomacy and cooperation between peoples".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel_peace_prize_460-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobel_peace_prize-460">[460]</a></sup> Obama accepted this award in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway on December 10, 2009, with "deep gratitude and great humility."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN:_Obama_acceptance_transcript_461-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN:_Obama_acceptance_transcript-461">[461]</a></sup> The award drew a mixture of praise and criticism from world leaders and media figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-462"><a href="#cite_note-462">[462]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-463"><a href="#cite_note-463">[463]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-464"><a href="#cite_note-464">[464]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-465"><a href="#cite_note-465">[465]</a></sup> Obama's peace prize was called a "stunning surprise" by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-466"><a href="#cite_note-466">[466]</a></sup> Obama is the fourth U.S. president to be awarded the Nobel Peace Prize and the third to become a Nobel laureate while in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-467"><a href="#cite_note-467">[467]</a></sup> Obama's Nobel Prize has been viewed skeptically in subsequent years, especially after the director of the Nobel Institute, <a href="/wiki/Geir_Lundestad" title="Geir Lundestad">Geir Lundestad</a>, said Obama's Peace Prize did not have the desired effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-468"><a href="#cite_note-468">[468]</a></sup>
%&|<h2><span id="Post-presidency_.282017.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-presidency_(2017–present)">Post-presidency (2017–present)</span></h2>
<p>Obama's presidency ended at noon on January 20, 2017, immediately following the <a href="/wiki/Inauguration_of_Donald_Trump" title="Inauguration of Donald Trump">inauguration</a> of his Republican successor, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>. After the inauguration, Obama lifted off on <a href="/wiki/Executive_One" title="Executive One">Executive One</a>, circled the White House, and flew to <a href="/wiki/Joint_Base_Andrews" title="Joint Base Andrews">Joint Base Andrews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-469"><a href="#cite_note-469">[469]</a></sup> The family currently rents a house in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalorama,_Washington,_D.C." title="Kalorama, Washington, D.C.">Kalorama, Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-470"><a href="#cite_note-470">[470]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 2, 2017, the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum">John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum</a> awarded the annual <a href="/wiki/Profile_in_Courage_Award" title="Profile in Courage Award">Profile in Courage Award</a> to Obama "for his enduring commitment to democratic ideals and elevating the standard of political courage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-471"><a href="#cite_note-471">[471]</a></sup> In his first public appearance out of office, Obama appeared at a seminar at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> on April 24. The seminar was aimed at the engagement with a new generation as well as an appeal for their participation in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-472"><a href="#cite_note-472">[472]</a></sup> On May 4, three days ahead of the <a href="/wiki/2017_French_presidential_election" title="2017 French presidential election">French presidential election</a>, Obama publicly endorsed <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron" title="Emmanuel Macron">Emmanuel Macron</a>: "He appeals to people's hopes and not their fears, and I enjoyed speaking to Emmanuel recently to hear about his independent movement and his vision for the future of France."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-473"><a href="#cite_note-473">[473]</a></sup> Macron went on to win the election.
</p><p>While in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> on May 25, Obama made a joint public appearance with Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> where he stressed inclusion and for leaders to question themselves, Obama having been formally invited to Berlin while still in office as part of an effort to boost Merkel's re-election campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-474"><a href="#cite_note-474">[474]</a></sup> Obama traveled to <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Palace" title="Kensington Palace">Kensington Palace</a> in England and met with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Harry" title="Prince Harry">Prince Harry</a> on May 27, 2017; Obama tweeted afterward that the two discussed their foundations and offering condolences in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2017_Manchester_Arena_bombing" title="2017 Manchester Arena bombing">Manchester Arena bombing</a> that occurred five days prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-475"><a href="#cite_note-475">[475]</a></sup>
</p><p>After President Trump announced his withdrawal of the United States from the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> on June 1, Obama released a statement disagreeing with the choice: "But even in the absence of American leadership; even as this administration joins a small handful of nations that reject the future; I'm confident that our states, cities, and businesses will step up and do even more to lead the way, and help protect for future generations the one planet we've got."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-476"><a href="#cite_note-476">[476]</a></sup>
<p>After Senate Republicans revealed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Better_Care_Reconciliation_Act_of_2017" title="Better Care Reconciliation Act of 2017">Better Care Reconciliation Act of 2017</a>, their discussion draft of a health care bill to replace the Affordable Care Act, on June 22, Obama released a Facebook post calling the bill "a massive transfer of wealth from middle-class and poor families to the richest people in America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-477"><a href="#cite_note-477">[477]</a></sup> On September 19, while delivering the keynote address at Goalkeepers, Obama admitted his frustration with Republicans backing "a bill that will raise costs, reduce coverage, and roll back protections for older Americans and people with pre-existing conditions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-478"><a href="#cite_note-478">[478]</a></sup>
</p><p>After Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Sessions" title="Jeff Sessions">Jeff Sessions</a> announced the termination of the <a href="/wiki/Deferred_Action_for_Childhood_Arrivals" title="Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals">Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals</a> (DACA) program on September 5, Obama released a Facebook post rebuking the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-479"><a href="#cite_note-479">[479]</a></sup> Two days later, he partnered with former presidents <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> to work with <a href="/wiki/One_America_Appeal" title="One America Appeal">One America Appeal</a> to help the victims of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Harvey" title="Hurricane Harvey">Hurricane Harvey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-480"><a href="#cite_note-480">[480]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama hosted the inaugural summit of the <a href="/wiki/Obama_Foundation" title="Obama Foundation">Obama Foundation</a> in Chicago on October 31. Obama intends for the foundation to be the central focus of his post-presidency and part of his ambitions for his subsequent activities following his presidency to be more consequential than his time in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-481"><a href="#cite_note-481">[481]</a></sup> Obama has also been working on a Presidential memoir, in a reported $65 million deal with <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Random_House" title="Penguin Random House">Penguin Random House</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nymag_482-0"><a href="#cite_note-nymag-482">[482]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama went on an international trip from November 28 to December 2, 2017, and visited China, India and France. In China, he delivered remarks at the Global Alliance of SMEs Summit in Shanghai and met with Chinese President <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> in Beijing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-483"><a href="#cite_note-483">[483]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-484"><a href="#cite_note-484">[484]</a></sup> He then went to India, where he spoke at the Hindustan Times Leadership Summit before meeting with Indian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Narendra_Modi" title="Narendra Modi">Narendra Modi</a> over lunch. In addition, he held a town hall for young leaders, organized by the Obama Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-485"><a href="#cite_note-485">[485]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-486"><a href="#cite_note-486">[486]</a></sup> He also met with the <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a> while in New Delhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-487"><a href="#cite_note-487">[487]</a></sup> He ended his five-day trip in France where he met with French President <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron" title="Emmanuel Macron">Emmanuel Macron</a>, former President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francois_Hollande" title="Francois Hollande">Francois Hollande</a> and Paris Mayor <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hidalgo" title="Anne Hidalgo">Anne Hidalgo</a> and then spoke at an invitation-only event, touching on climate issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-488"><a href="#cite_note-488">[488]</a></sup>
</p><p>Barack and Michelle Obama signed a deal on May 22, 2018 to produce docu-series, documentaries and features for <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> under the Obama's newly formed production company, Higher Ground Productions. On the deal, Michelle said "I have always believed in the power of storytelling to inspire us, to make us think differently about the world around us, and to help us open our minds and hearts to others".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-489"><a href="#cite_note-489">[489]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-490"><a href="#cite_note-490">[490]</a></sup>
</p><p>A package that contained a <a href="/wiki/October_2018_United_States_mail_bombing_attempts" title="October 2018 United States mail bombing attempts">pipe bomb</a> was sent to Obama's home in Washington, D.C, on October 24, 2018. The package was intercepted by the Secret Service during routine mail screenings. Similar packages were sent to several other Democratic leaders, mostly those who voiced strong objections to the policies of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> and to <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>. <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Wasserman_Schultz" title="Debbie Wasserman Schultz">Debbie Wasserman Schultz</a> was addressed as the sender of the package. On October 26, 2018, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesar_Sayoc" title="Cesar Sayoc">Cesar Sayoc</a> was arrested and faces five federal charges in Manhattan carrying a combined maximum sentence of 48 years behind bars in relation to the pipe bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-491"><a href="#cite_note-491">[491]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-492"><a href="#cite_note-492">[492]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, Barack and Michelle Obama bought a home on <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wyc_Grousbeck" title="Wyc Grousbeck">Wyc Grousbeck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-493"><a href="#cite_note-493">[493]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-494"><a href="#cite_note-494">[494]</a></sup>
%&|<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<p>Obama's most significant legacy is generally considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act</a> (PPACA), provisions of which went into effect from 2010 to 2020. Many attempts by Senate Republicans to repeal the PPACA, including a "skinny repeal", have thus far failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_495-0"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-495">[495]</a></sup> Together with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_Care_and_Education_Reconciliation_Act" title="Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act">Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act</a> amendment, it represents the <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">U.S. healthcare system</a>'s most significant regulatory overhaul and expansion of coverage since the passage of <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oberlander2010_496-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oberlander2010-496">[496]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blumenthal2015_497-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blumenthal2015-497">[497]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CohenEtAl_498-0"><a href="#cite_note-CohenEtAl-498">[498]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTsigning_499-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTsigning-499">[499]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many commentators credit Obama with averting a threatened <a href="/wiki/Depression_(economics)" title="Depression (economics)">depression</a> and pulling the economy back from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_495-1"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-495">[495]</a></sup> According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics">U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_administration" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a> created 11.3 million jobs from the month after <a href="/wiki/First_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="First inauguration of Barack Obama">his first inauguration</a> to the end of his term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500"><a href="#cite_note-500">[500]</a></sup> In 2010, Obama signed into effect the <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Wall_Street_Reform_and_Consumer_Protection_Act" title="Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act">Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act</a>. Passed as a response to the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">financial crisis of 2007–08</a>, it brought the most significant changes to <a href="/wiki/Financial_regulation" title="Financial regulation">financial regulation</a> in the United States since the regulatory reform that followed the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> under Democratic President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-501"><a href="#cite_note-501">[501]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, Obama signed into law the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Authorization_Act_for_Fiscal_Year_2010" title="National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2010">National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2010</a>, which contained in it the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Shepard_and_James_Byrd_Jr._Hate_Crimes_Prevention_Act" title="Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act">Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act</a>, the first addition to existing federal hate crime law in the United States since Democratic President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> signed into law the Church Arson Prevention Act of 1996. The Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act expanded <a href="/wiki/Hate_crime_laws_in_the_United_States" title="Hate crime laws in the United States">existing federal hate crime laws in the United States</a> to apply to crimes motivated by a victim's actual or perceived gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability, and dropped the prerequisite that the victim be engaging in a federally protected activity.
</p><p>As president, Obama advanced LGBT rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-502"><a href="#cite_note-502">[502]</a></sup> In 2010, he signed the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Ask,_Don%27t_Tell_Repeal_Act_of_2010" title="Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010">Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act</a>, which brought an end to "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">don't ask, don't tell</a>" policy in the U.S. armed forces that banned open service from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lesbian,_gay_and_bisexual" title="Lesbian, gay and bisexual">LGB</a> people; the law went into effect the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-503"><a href="#cite_note-503">[503]</a></sup> In 2016, his administration brought an end to the ban on <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> people serving openly in the U.S. armed forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-504"><a href="#cite_note-504">[504]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TransBan1_219-1"><a href="#cite_note-TransBan1-219">[219]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallup_poll" title="Gallup poll">Gallup poll</a>, taken in the final days of Obama's term, showed that 68% of Americans believed the U.S. had made progress in the situation for gays and lesbians during Obama's eight years in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-505"><a href="#cite_note-505">[505]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama substantially escalated the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drone_strikes" title="Drone strikes">drone strikes</a> against suspected militants and terrorists associated with <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">al-Qaeda</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-506"><a href="#cite_note-506">[506]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-507"><a href="#cite_note-507">[507]</a></sup> In 2016, the last year of his presidency, the US dropped 26,171 bombs on seven different countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-508"><a href="#cite_note-508">[508]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-509"><a href="#cite_note-509">[509]</a></sup> Obama left about 8,400 US troops in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, 5,262 in Iraq, 503 in Syria, 133 in Pakistan, 106 in Somalia, seven in Yemen, and two in Libya at the end of his presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-510"><a href="#cite_note-510">[510]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-511"><a href="#cite_note-511">[511]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Justice_Statistics" title="United States Bureau of Justice Statistics">United States Bureau of Justice Statistics</a>, from December 31, 2009, to December 31, 2015, inmates sentenced in US federal custody declined by five percent. This is the largest decline in sentenced inmates in US federal custody since Democratic President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>. By contrast, the federal prison population increased significantly under presidents <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-512"><a href="#cite_note-512">[512]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama left office in January 2017 with a 60% approval rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-513"><a href="#cite_note-513">[513]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-514"><a href="#cite_note-514">[514]</a></sup> A 2018 survey of historians by the <a href="/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association" title="American Political Science Association">American Political Science Association</a> ranked Obama the <a href="/wiki/Historical_rankings_of_presidents_of_the_United_States" title="Historical rankings of presidents of the United States">8th-greatest American President</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_Does_Trump_Stack_Up_Against_the_Best—and_Worst—Presidents?_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-How_Does_Trump_Stack_Up_Against_the_Best—and_Worst—Presidents?-4">[4]</a></sup> Obama gained 10 spots from the same survey in 2015 from the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">Brookings Institution</a> that ranked Obama the 18th-greatest American President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-515"><a href="#cite_note-515">[515]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
<p>The Barack Obama Presidential Center is Obama's planned <a href="/wiki/Presidential_library" title="Presidential library">presidential library</a>. It will be hosted by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> and located in <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Park_(Chicago)" title="Jackson Park (Chicago)">Jackson Park</a> on the <a href="/wiki/South_Side,_Chicago" title="South Side, Chicago">South Side of Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-516"><a href="#cite_note-516">[516]</a></sup>
